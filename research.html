Flagship study

ML-guided tungsten single atoms for noble-metal-free water electrolysis

My first-author Nature Communications (2026) study is the clearest example of how I work. I fine-tuned EquiformerV2 on custom DFT datasets to screen 3,976 single-atom oxyhydroxide configurations, identified W₁-NiFeOOH as a promising catalyst, and synthesized it through scalable cyclic electrodeposition. I then resolved its mechanism with operando Raman and synchrotron XAS, and translated the catalyst into an anion-exchange-membrane electrolyzer that reached 13.1 A cm⁻² at 2.0 V under practical conditions — atomistic prediction, interfacial mechanism, and device-relevant operation connected in a single closed loop.

Beyond water splitting

The same theory-to-device approach carries across CO₂ electroreduction, glycerol and oxygenated-feed upgrading, acidic enol electrooxidation, and electrocatalytic ammonia oxidation. In my current postdoctoral work I am developing a multiscale computational framework — coupling DFT, machine-learned interatomic potentials, and molecular dynamics with explicit solvent — to model ion transport within anion-exchange membranes and ionomer layers, validated through MEA fabrication and testing. I am also setting up and evaluating direct CO₂ capture systems, toward integrating capture and electrochemical conversion rather than treating them as separate processes.
