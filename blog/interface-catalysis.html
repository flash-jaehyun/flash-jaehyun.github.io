ELECTROCATALYSIS · INTERFACIAL SCIENCE Research Brief · Rev 2026.07.19

The nanoparticle is only half the catalyst.

Across CO2 reduction, oxygen electrocatalysis and organic oxidations, the last three years of work converge on one claim: activity and selectivity are set as much by the electrolyte packed against the particle — its cations, anions, pH and water — as by the metal itself. This brief synthesizes the current mechanistic picture and the design rules that fall out of it.

DISTINCT WAYS THE DOUBLE LAYER ACTS

Competitive adsorption, potential-drop redistribution, dipole–field coupling, ion–intermediate chemistry, interfacial pH buffering and water restructuring — interrelated, often acting at once.

I

The interface is the reactor

The electrical double layer (EDL) has been known for a century to shift observed rates, but a full account has stayed out of reach because so many components interact at once. A 2024 Chemical Reviews synthesis (Schott et al., 124, 12391) reframed the field around assessing and predicting EDL properties directly, rather than treating them as an empirical nuisance. The reaction does not happen “on” the metal so much as inside a few ångströms of structured ions and water.

A useful inventory comes from combined experiment–theory work on Pt (Nature Communications, 2022), which enumerates six interrelated phenomena that operate within the double-layer microenvironment. Design intent means choosing which of these to pull on.

II

Cations, under one electrostatic law

For years cation effects looked reaction-specific and contradictory. Resasco's 2025 perspective, A Universal Model of Cation Effects (JACS Au), argues they are all one thing: cations set the strength of the interfacial electric field, and that field shifts the energy of adsorbates and transition states in proportion to their dipole moments and polarizabilities. Cations that pack more densely steepen the potential drop and raise the field.

Two conditions follow, and they are testable: (1) the operating potential must be negative of the electrode's potential of zero total charge (PZTC), so cations actually accumulate; and (2) the kinetically relevant step must be field-sensitive. Multiscale modelling of CO2-to-CO on Ag reproduces rate vs. cation identity, CO2 pressure and concentration with no empirical fit parameters — small hydrated Cs+ and K+ pack tight, build large fields, and polarize Stern-layer water (ACS Catalysis, 2025).

The same logic governs the messy cases. In acidic CO2 reduction on cobalt phthalocyanine, the proper cations act as an electrostatic shield that blocks bulk H+ from reaching the surface while reshaping interfacial water to suppress hydrogen evolution (J. Phys. Chem. Lett., 2024).

III

Anions, pH & the concentration lever

Anions and cations are not independent. Multiscale modelling shows larger cations such as Cs+ shield anions from the cathode, relieving their inhibition of CO2 adsorption (ACS Catalysis, 2025). The two ion effects have to be read together, which is why single-variable electrolyte studies so often disagree.

Concentration is the most under-used knob. On a single unchanged Cu gas-diffusion electrode, moving from 1 M to 7.1 M HCOOK lifts the ethylene-to-CO Faradaic-efficiency ratio from 2.2 to 18.3 at −1.08 V; ATR-SEIRAS attributes it to a dominant *CO-bridge binding mode, and the authors conclude ion identity and concentration outrank bulk CO2 solubility and bulk pH (ChemRxiv, 2024).

On the anodic side, anion identity is equally live: on Pt across the ORR/OER window, ClO4− binds weakest, then NO3−, then SO42−, pointing to nitrate-like microenvironments as lower-poisoning alternatives to sulfonate ionomers (Nature Communications, 2022). And in near-neutral OER, NiOOH shows an intrinsic cation effect independent of Fe impurities, mediated by stabilization of the NiOO− intermediate (Natl. Sci. Rev., 2024).

IV

Water is not the solvent — it is a reagent

Interfacial water governs reaction kinetics, proton and electron transfer, and mass transport at the junction (Angew. Chem., 2025). A 2025 EES Catalysis review classifies its roles into four active parts: co-catalyst, masking agent, regulator of reaction intermediates, and inducer of catalyst reconfiguration (EES Catal., 3, 943). It is no longer background.

The mechanism is the hydrogen-bond network. Its connectivity in the double layer — not any intrinsic metal property — dominates the kinetic pH effect in hydrogen electrocatalysis on Pt (Nat. Catal., 2022); a more connected network moves protons faster. Because water structure is what cations, fields and facets ultimately reorganize, it is the common variable underneath the others.

V

The particle still writes the product

None of this retires the catalyst. On copper, facet is destiny: the (100) surface drives C–C coupling toward C2 products while (111) steers toward CH4, and this structure–selectivity link survives down to the nanoscale, further tunable by size (ACS Catalysis, 2020). Shape-controlled nanocrystals turn that into a dial.

Size cuts two ways. DFT on sub-2 nm clusters finds Cu38 and Cu79 optimal for C2 formation with minimal overpotential (Nanoscale, 2025), and step-edge-anchored 2–5 nm Cu particles lower overpotential and improve selectivity (Commun. Chem., 2024). The catch, imported from decades of ORR work: idealized single-crystal performance rarely reproduces inside a real membrane-electrode assembly — the interface that surrounds the particle at 200 mA cm−2 is not the interface of an H-cell.

VI

The rule generalizes beyond CO2

Interface engineering is not a CO2RR curiosity. The same levers — potential, local field, heterointerface, waveform — steer selectivity in oxygen electrocatalysis and in organic oxidations where over-oxidation and C–C cleavage compete.

ORR / OER
Surface charge as a screening filter

The PZTC criterion lets you predict, before any test, which oxygen-catalyst surfaces will even respond to electrolyte tuning — and it now screens thousands of transition-metal-oxide compositions computationally.

GLYCEROL OXIDATION
Waveform & heterointerface pick the C

Pulsed potential steers glycerol to glyceric acid (Nat. Commun., 2024); a NiOOH/CeO2 nanoheterointerface reaches 95.7% FE to formate via selective C–C cleavage (Nano Lett.).

AMMONIA OXIDATION
Site pairing routes N to N2

Diatomic and alloy sites tune the N-intermediate binding that decides N2-vs-NOx selectivity; interface control is central to the emerging AOR literature (Chem Catal., 2024).

PAIRED ELECTROLYSIS
Both interfaces earn their keep

Coupling a productive anodic oxidation (biomass, plastic, ammonia synthesis) to cathodic reduction reframes the anode interface as a value stream, not a waste-heat OER (Chem Catal., 2025).

DESIGN RULES · 설계 규칙

Six ways to tune the interface, not just the metal

RULE 01
Choose the cation by PZTC

If the operating potential is negative of the surface's PZTC and the key step is field-sensitive, larger cations win. If not, don't bother.

RULE 02
Concentrate before you redesign

High salt concentration can deliver an order-of-magnitude selectivity shift on an unchanged electrode. Try it first.

RULE 03
Match facet to bond

Want C–C coupling? Expose {100}. Want a C1 product? Expose {111}. Shape control is selectivity control.

RULE 04
Raise EDL capacitance

High-dielectric surroundings (e.g. mica on Ag) enrich local reactant and pushed CO FE to 98% — a physical, not chemical, edit.

RULE 05
Shield protons in acid

Cations can form an electrostatic barrier to bulk H+, buying CO2RR selectivity in acidic media where HER would otherwise win.

RULE 06
Engineer the water network

Connectivity of interfacial H-bonds sets proton-transfer rate. It is the variable the other five ultimately move.

WHAT THE EVIDENCE DOES NOT YET SETTLE
Coupled ion effects

Cation–anion interactions are still hard to deconvolute in real multi-component electrolytes; individual contributions resist isolation.

Acid stability

Acidic CO2RR relieves carbonate loss but catalyst dissolution and dynamic microenvironment drift remain unresolved.

Lab-to-device gap

Single-crystal and H-cell results routinely fail to transfer to MEA reactors; interfacial conditions differ fundamentally at scale.
